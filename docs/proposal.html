Proposal

1. Dataset & Research Questions

License

The “Arbeitsmarkt - Arbeitslosen” dataset uses Datenlizenz Deutschland Namensnennung 2.0 (dl-by-de) (Version 2.0). This is a German open government licence: you can copy, use, and redistribute the data for any purpose, including commercially, as long as you attribute the source.

Source

https://www.mstatistik-muenchen.de/indikatorenatlas/export/export.php

What is in it

Rows: 3250

Columns: 10

Each row represents the yearly percentage of unemployed people per district in Munich, as well as the total yearly percentage across all districts, further divided into total, German, non-German, male, and female categories.

Columns include the id, indicator name, unemployed-person type (total, male, female, …), year, Munich district (including the city total), unemployment percentage, unemployed count, and total resident count (15–64 years old).

Row identification: year (Jahr), district (Raumbezug), and demographic group (Auspragung) together uniquely identify each row.

Key variables we expect to use are the year, district, unemployment percentage, and both base values.

Initial documentation gaps in raw file

Indikatorwert: the indicator value — not obvious from the name alone that this is the unemployment percentage, or which unit is missing (%).

Basiswert 1 / Basiswert 2: the names give no information about what the values mean.

Auspragung: the column name gives no information about the values it contains — a categorical variable for demographic/subpopulation groups.

Structural gaps

The Munich Open Data page gives a formula description but no real variable dictionary. Columns like Auspragung and Basiswert 1 / Basiswert 2 are left unexplained.

A separate metadata PDF from the Statistisches Amt München (https://stadt.muenchen.de/dam/jcr:42312f77-cae6-4995-a534-ff9dfff69480/indikatorenatlas_muenchen_metadaten_2023.pdf) reveals information not present in the file itself: Basiswert 2 changes its meaning depending on Auspragung (whether the row concerns gender or nationality), and “Stadt München” rows aggregate geographic data differently than individual district rows.

A user who loads the dataset without tracking down this external PDF could misinterpret the metrics or miscalculate averages by mixing districts and city totals.

Research Questions

  1. How has the unemployment percentage in Munich changed from 2000 to 2024 overall and by city area (North, East, South, West, Center)? → Descriptive
  2. Do unemployment percentages differ in 2024 based on gender across districts in South Munich? → Explanatory

Cut:

We originally considered a third, predictive research question (“Can we build a simple model to predict the unemployment percentage in the next years?”). We cut this to keep scope focused on the two questions above. Also the updated proposal required to lock in at most two focused research questions.

Context and target audience

Target audience: Agentur für Arbeit München, Jobcenter München, Referat für Arbeit und Wirtschaft der Landeshauptstadt München.

Purpose: analysis of trends in each district to plan resources and aid for unemployed citizens, and to design additional support such as seminars tailored to each district.

Presentation: statistical report

Why we chose the dataset

The dataset contains the unemployment percentage for each district and for the city of Munich overall. It lets us examine each district and the trend of the unemployment rate over 24 years. The advantage over raw unemployment counts is that the percentage immediately shows the relation to population size. The analysis matters because it shows the history of Munich’s unemployment and how different years and policy choices coincide with different outcomes for the city.

3. Analysis Plan

RQ1: Unemployment trend by city area (2000–2024)

Specific claim: We estimate the trend of mean unemployment percentage over time (Indikatorwert ~ Jahr) grouped by city area and total, by calculating the annual average of the districts, and report line graphs showing the trend from 2000 to 2024.

Classify the analysis: Goal: Descriptive — we describe the state of Indikatorwert per city area from 2000–2024. Exploratory — after reviewing the available variables we fixed this question.

Model specification:

  • Response: Indikatorwert — unemployment percentage in % (<dbl>)
  • Predictor: Jahr — year (<dbl>), across six groups of Raumbezug (North, East, South, West, Center, Total)
  • Method: annual mean of the districts within each city area and city-wide total
  • Expected output: a summary dataset of annual means and line plots showing the trend from 2000 to 2024
  • Interpretation: this tells us how the unemployment rate has historically developed and shifted over a 24-year period across different regions of Munich

District → area mapping. Munich’s 25 districts don’t come pre-labeled with a compass region, so we assigned each district to North / East / South / West based on its approximate position relative to the city center. The two central districts (Altstadt-Lehel, Ludwigsvorstadt-Isarvorstadt) are excluded from the area breakdown since they sit at the center rather than in any one direction; they remain included in the city-wide total.

Note: this mapping is a judgment call we made, not an official designation.

Show code 
district_area <- tibble::tibble(
  district_code = c(
    1, 2, 3,                     # Center
    4, 11, 12, 24,                # North
    5, 13, 14, 15, 16,            # East
    6, 7, 17, 18, 19, 20,         # South
    8, 9, 10, 21, 22, 23, 25      # West
  ),
  area = c(
    rep("Center", 3),
    rep("North", 4),
    rep("East", 5),
    rep("South", 6),
    rep("West", 7)
  )
)

arbeitslosen_area <- arbeitslosen %>%
  mutate(district_code = as.numeric(str_extract(Raumbezug, "^[0-9]+"))) %>%
  left_join(district_area, by = "district_code") %>%
  filter(!is.na(area), Auspragung == "insgesamt")

annual_means_area <- arbeitslosen_area %>%
  group_by(area, Jahr) %>%
  summarise(mean_rate = mean(Indikatorwert, na.rm = TRUE), .groups = "drop")

annual_means_total <- arbeitslosen %>%
  filter(Auspragung == "insgesamt", Raumbezug == "Stadt München") %>%
  transmute(area = "Total", Jahr, mean_rate = Indikatorwert)

annual_means_all <- bind_rows(annual_means_area, annual_means_total)

Figure 4: Interactive version of Figure 3: hover over any point to see the exact year and mean unemployment rate for that area

The interactive version lets readers hover over any point to read the exact year and mean unemployment rate for a given city area, rather than estimating values from the line alone.

Description: All five regions and the city total show a similar trend. In 2000 all graphs except the center area graph start at around 3.6% and drastically peak in 2005 with almost 7% and only a small decline in 2004. The center area graph has a similar trend but only peaks at around 6% in 2005. After 2005 all graphs show an inconsistent but almost identical decline to about 2.5% in 2019. In 2020 every area graph shows a drastic jump to about 4%, declines again in 2022 but increases from then on to 4% in 2024.

Interpretation: The peak in 2005 is explained by a newly introduced reform Hartz IV the same year that counted the unemployed and people on welfare into one number. After this, new rules were introduced where receiving welfare became stricter and so the numbers decreased again.

RQ2: Gender differences in South Munich, 2024

Specific claim: We compare mean unemployment percentage between männlich and weiblich groups (Indikatorwert ~ Auspragung) across South Munich districts for the year 2024, using a difference-in-means test, and report the estimated difference, a 95% confidence interval, and a p-value.

Classify the analysis: Goal: Explanatory — we ask whether Auspragung (gender) is related to Indikatorwert. Exploratory — after seeing the data was already grouped by gender, we decided to investigate further.

Model specification:

  • Response: Indikatorwert — unemployment percentage in % (<dbl>)
  • Predictor: Auspragung — categorical grouping filtered to männlich & weiblich (<chr>)
  • Method: difference in means between the two groups, using a theory-based t null distribution
  • Expected output: the observed difference in means, a null-distribution plot with the observed statistic shaded, a p-value, and a 95% CI
  • Interpretation: this tells us whether male residents face systematically higher or lower unemployment percentages compared to female residents across South Munich districts in 2024

{infer} test

Show code 
library(infer)

south_districts <- c(6, 7, 17, 18, 19, 20)

arbeitslosen_south <- arbeitslosen |>
  mutate(district_code = as.numeric(str_extract(Raumbezug, "^[0-9]+"))) |>
  filter(district_code %in% south_districts)

obs_stat <- arbeitslosen_south |>
  filter(Jahr == 2024 & Auspragung %in% c("männlich", "weiblich")) |>
  specify(Indikatorwert ~ Auspragung) |>
  calculate(
    stat = "diff in means",
    order = c("männlich", "weiblich")
  )

null_dist <- arbeitslosen_south |>
  filter(Auspragung %in% c("männlich", "weiblich")) |>
  specify(Indikatorwert ~ Auspragung) |>
  assume("t")

null_dist |>
  visualize() +
  shade_p_value(obs_stat = obs_stat, direction = "both")

Result interpretation: the difference in average unemployment rate between männlich and weiblich in 2024 falls within the high-probability region of the null distribution. The variation between groups can be explained by random sampling variation, and we cannot reject the null hypothesis that there is no systematic gender gap.

4. Workflow Organisation

Code style

  • tidyverse style guide: <- for assignments, spaces around operators, …
  • shared AI system prompt so output follows tidyverse conventions

Packages

  • tidyverse
  • readr
  • naniar, skimr

We initialize renv in our project repo to create renv.lock, so that our package versions are locked down and our code runs identically across the group.

Git workflow

We create separate branches per task (e.g. feature/write-IDA) and have the other group member review before merging into main. This lets the other group member pull finished tasks and avoids merge conflicts and messy files that a single shared branch would cause.
